THE PROBLEM

Building custom fishing rods requires a spinner that rotates the blank slowly and steadily for hours while epoxy cures over the thread wraps. Without one, the resin sags and pools instead of curing evenly — and the rod is ruined.

Commercial rod spinners run $400–$600. That's a hard sell for a tool that's fundamentally a slow-turning motor on a stick. There had to be a better way.

// WHAT WENT WRONG

ITERATION LOG

3 redesigns  ·  1 working spinner

// ISSUE 01
SPEED CONTROL

First motor spun way too fast on every battery tried — AA, AAA, 9V. Resistors couldn't slow it down enough. With a rod loaded, AA/AAA barely turned it; 9V slowly built speed then whipped the off-balance rod around.

// FIX

Swapped to a geared DC motor with built-in reduction. Slow RPM by design, plus enough torque to start on its own and hold steady under load.

// ISSUE 02
WOBBLE AT HOLDER

First 3D-printed holder used four bolts with heat-set nuts to clamp the rod butt — like a Christmas tree stand. The hardware worked, but four bolt heads threw the holder off-balance and made the whole assembly wobble badly.

// FIX

Switched to smaller, lighter screws. Same clamping force, way less mass. No wobble.

// ISSUE 03
MOTOR COUPLING

The 3D-printed holder needed to mount cleanly to the motor shaft to keep the spin true. Designing a precision coupler from scratch — a part that transmits torque without running out — was its own engineering problem.

// FIX

The geared motor came with a small output wheel. Hot-glued that wheel to the center of the rod holder — instant centered mount, clean spin.

// INSPIRATION

WHY THIS PROJECT

I love to fish and I love to build things. Most of my projects pick one or the other — this one sits right in the middle. Custom rod-building has always interested me, and the spinner was the missing piece between wanting to do it and actually being able to.

// IMPACT

WHAT THIS UNLOCKS

This spinner replaces a $600 commercial unit at a fraction of the cost and unlocks the ability to build custom rods at home. Beyond the savings, the build itself was the point — designing a functional part in Fusion 360, troubleshooting alignment issues, and ending up with a tool I'll actually use.
